About the Park

Parque de las Américas is one of the most iconic public spaces in Mérida—a place where architecture, history, and everyday life blend into a uniquely meaningful urban landmark. Located in the García Ginerés neighborhood, the park stands out not only for its size and beauty, but also for the cultural symbolism embedded in its design. The park was built between 1942 and 1945 during the administration of Governor Ernesto Novelo Torres, on land that had previously been used for other unfinished projects, including a monument dedicated to Felipe Carrillo Puerto. Its design was entrusted to the architects Manuel Amábilis Domínguez and Max Amábilis Rhodon, who conceived the space as a tribute to the unity of the American continent. This vision is reflected in the park’s layout, where each country of the Americas is represented through carved stone stelae featuring names, maps, and national symbols. Architecturally, the park is especially significant for its neomaya style, a regional interpretation of modern architecture that incorporates elements inspired by ancient Mayan design. Structures such as the concha acústica (open-air theater), the central fountain, and the José Martí Cultural Center all echo forms found in sites like Chichén Itzá, while blending them with Art Deco influences. This fusion makes the park a rare and cohesive example of 20th-century nationalist architecture in Mexico. Beyond its design, the importance of Parque de las Américas lies in its role as a social and cultural hub. Since its inauguration, it has served as a gathering place for families, artists, and the community at large, hosting cultural events, performances, and everyday recreation. Over time, it has evolved from a simple public space into a symbol of identity and collective memory for the city, often described as a “living heritage” that reflects Mérida’s history and values. In essence, Parque de las Américas is more than just a park—it is a carefully designed monument to cultural unity, a showcase of regional architectural identity, and one of Mérida’s most enduring historic landmarks.
